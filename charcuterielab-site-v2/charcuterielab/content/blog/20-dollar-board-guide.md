The single biggest misconception in charcuterie is that impressive boards require expensive ingredients. They don't. They require intentional ones — and a few technique principles that take 60 seconds to learn and change everything about how your board looks.

Here's the exact $20 board, the methodology, and why it works.

## The Ingredient List

**From any grocery store, under $20 total:**

- Aged cheddar block (8 oz) — $4–6
- Summer sausage (7 oz package) — $4–5
- Fig jam (small jar) — $3–4
- Assorted crackers (one box, varied shapes if possible) — $3–4
- Red seedless grapes (small bunch) — $2–3
- Honey (a few tablespoons from whatever you have) — $0 if you own it

Total: $16–22 depending on store and location.

## The Anchor-and-Fill Method

This is the technique that separates a scattered plate of food from a *board*.

**Step 1: Place your anchors first.** Anchors are your largest items — the cheese block (cut one wedge off the corner so there's a "tasting face"), a small ramekin of fig jam, and a small cluster of the summer sausage folded into half-moons. These go down first, spaced apart across the board to distribute visual weight.

**Step 2: Place crackers in fanned lines.** Run crackers in diagonal lines outward from the cheese. The fan shape fills space efficiently and gives the board directional energy.

**Step 3: Fill gaps with grapes.** Small grape clusters fill irregular gaps better than any other ingredient because they're shapeable. Push them into corners and odd spaces.

**Step 4: Add honey last.** Drizzle a small amount directly over the cheese, not in a separate bowl. This connects the cheese visually to the surrounding board and signals to guests that the honey-cheese combination is intentional.

## The Height Principle

Flat boards look like a plate. Boards with height look designed.

Create height by: leaving the cheese block largely whole instead of pre-slicing it entirely, stacking 2–3 crackers slightly upright against the cheese, and leaving the grape clusters intact rather than pulling individual grapes.

The goal is variation — no section of the board should be at the same height as the section next to it.

## Why This Board Works Flavor-wise

These five ingredients hit every major flavor category a good board needs:

- **Salty/savory:** summer sausage, aged cheddar
- **Sweet:** fig jam, grapes, honey
- **Fatty/rich:** cheddar, sausage fat
- **Acidic/fresh:** grapes (tartaric acid)
- **Textural contrast:** crunchy crackers vs. soft jam vs. tender sausage vs. firm cheddar

Every cracker a guest builds contains at least three of these elements. The balance is built in.
